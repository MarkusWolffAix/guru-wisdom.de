# Is Urfa the Biblical Ur?

## The First Temple of Humankind

The question of whether the modern Turkish city of Urfa (officially Şanlıurfa) is identical to the biblical city of Ur (called "Ur in Chaldea" or "Ur Kasdim" in the Old Testament) has long occupied historians, theologians, and archaeologists. The city of Ur is known in the Bible as the birthplace of the patriarch Abraham (Ibrahim).

Here are the most important connections and arguments linking Urfa to the biblical Ur:

## 1. Geographical and Logistical Proximity to Haran
One of the strongest arguments for Urfa being the biblical Ur is geography.

* **The Biblical Travel Narrative:** According to Genesis, Abraham's family moved from "Ur in Chaldea" to Haran and later on to Canaan.

* **The Short Distance:** Haran (modern Harran) is located only about 45 kilometers southeast of Urfa. Moving from Urfa to Haran is a short, logical step.

* **The Problem of the Southern Theory:** The currently favored archaeological theory equates biblical Ur with the Sumerian city of Ur in southern Iraq (Tell el-Muqayyar). Traveling from there to Canaan and making a "stopover" in Haran, located far to the north, would have been an enormous and illogical detour of over 1,000 kilometers.

## 2. Strong Local and Islamic Traditions
In local culture as well as in Islam, the identification of Urfa with Abraham's birthplace is deeply rooted.

* **City of Prophets:** Urfa is popularly known as the "City of Prophets."

* **The Legend of Nimrod and the Fire:** According to Islamic and Jewish tradition, Abraham was born in Urfa and came into conflict there with the cruel King Nimrod. When Nimrod had Abraham thrown into the fire, God transformed the fire into water and the glowing logs into fish.

* **Balıklıgöl:** This alleged site of the miracle is known today as Balıklıgöl (Abraham's Fishpond) and is one of the most important pilgrimage sites in Urfa. A nearby cave is also venerated as Abraham's birthplace.

## 3. Linguistic Connections
The ancient name of Urfa also offers clues to a connection.

* In antiquity (among other languages, Syriac/Aramaic), the city was called **Urhai** or **Orhai**.

* Many linguists and historians point out that the name "Ur" could be a shortened form or variation of this original name, "Urhai."

## 4. The Riddle of the "Chaldeans" (Kazdim)

The Bible speaks of "Ur in Chaldea" (Ur Kasdim).


* **The Southern Theory:** The Chaldeans lived in southern Mesopotamia (Iraq) during the 1st millennium BCE. This supports the theory of Sumerian Ur. However, according to biblical chronology, Abraham lived in the south for many centuries before the Chaldeans appeared.

* **The Northern Theory:** Some scholars argue that the term "Chaldeans" (Kasdim) in older texts could refer to groups of people who lived in northern Mesopotamia—that is, in the region of Urfa and Haran—or that it is a later editorial adaptation in the biblical text.

**Conclusion:** While Western archaeology, since Sir Leonard Woolley's excavations in the 1920s, has often considered Sumerian Ur in southern Iraq to be Abraham's homeland, the geographical route to Haran, linguistic roots, and millennia-old local traditions strongly support Urfa (Urhai) in the north.

# Historical Evolution of Şanlıurfa's Name

The city has a rich and varied history, reflected in its different names.

## Chronological Overview

| Era | Name | Background |

| :--- | :--- | :--- |

| **Pre-Antiquity** | **Urhay / Orrhai** | Original Aramaic name; later Syriac *Urhoy*. |

| **Hellenism** | **Edessa** | Named by Seleucus I Nicator after the Macedonian capital. |

**Seleucids** | **Antioch on the Kallyrhoea** | Nickname due to the city's prominent springs. |

**Early Islamic Period** | **ar-Ruha** | Arabic form, derived from the Aramaic *Urhay*. |

**Ottoman Empire** | **Urfa** | Shortened form of *ar-Ruha*, retained in Turkish. |

**Modern Era (since 1984)** | **Şanlıurfa** | Honorary title *şanlı* ("glorious") for resistance during the War of Independence. |

## Details on the Naming

### Edessa
This name remained the primary designation, especially in Western historiography and during the **Crusades** (County of Edessa).


### The Title "Şanlı"

The renaming took place on **June 12, 1984** by the Turkish Parliament. This honored the city for its fight against the French occupation in 1920 – similar to the neighboring cities of **Gazi**antep ("Fighter Antep") and **Kahraman**maraş ("Heroes Maraş").

### Religious Significance
In Jewish and Islamic contexts, the city is often identified with the biblical **Ur** (the home of Abraham), which is why it also bears the unofficial nickname *"City of Prophets"*.
