# Rudolf I of Habsburg

## The Rise of a Dynasty

### 1. The Historical Figures and Their Context
Rudolf I of Habsburg (1218–1291) was the first Holy Roman Emperor from the House of Habsburg. Often referred to as the "poor count," he came from the regional nobility of southwestern Germany (Alsace, Breisgau, northern Switzerland). The legend that he was necessarily born at Limburg Castle on the Kaiserstuhl is a later, glorifying historical account by the Fugger family and cannot be substantiated by contemporary sources.

His political rise culminated in his surprising election as king on October 1, 1273. After the chaos of the Interregnum, the electors were looking for a less "inconvenient" candidate whom they could control. A driving force behind this election was Engelbert II of Falkenburg, the Archbishop of Cologne. Engelbert sought a strong ally for his own conflicts—he was a combative ruler who was even imprisoned in a cage by the citizens of Cologne for a time.

### 2. Reign and Turning Points
The most important phase of Rudolf's reign was marked by the conflict with Ottokar II of Bohemia. In the Battle of Marchfeld (1278), Rudolf defeated his powerful rival. This victory secured Habsburg control over significant territories, including Austria and Styria—a milestone for the Habsburgs' later global power.

An important historical misconception concerns Rudolf's alleged relationship with the Ottoman Empire. During Rudolf's lifetime (he died in 1291), the Ottomans did not yet exist as a relevant power; the Ottoman Empire was only founded around 1299. The foreign policy concerns of his time were more focused on the Mongols in the east.

### 3. Geography of Power: Styria, Itinerant Kingship, and Speyer
Styria, often called the "Green Heart of Austria," became the central pillar of the Habsburg dynasty's power base through Rudolf's victory. Its geography is largely defined by the Mur River, which rises in the Hohe Tauern mountains, flows across Styria (and its capital, Graz), and finally empties into the Drava River.

Despite his victory in the east, Rudolf remained a typical medieval itinerant king. He had no fixed capital but ruled from horseback. His centers of power lay in his ancestral lands in the southwest and in the major imperial cities of southern Germany and the Rhineland. Only after 1278 did he spend an extended period consolidating his power in Vienna.

Rudolf died in 1291 in Germersheim. At his express wish, his body was buried in Speyer Cathedral. Speyer, located in Rhineland-Palatinate on the left bank of the Rhine, was the traditional burial place of the Salian and Hohenstaufen emperors. Through this burial, Rudolf symbolically placed himself among the great emperors and solidified the Habsburgs' dynastic claim even beyond his death.

### 4. Musical Memorial: "Count in a Gray Coat"
As a tribute to this eventful history, a piece of music in the style of "Neo-Classical Folk" was composed. Since the piece was originally written in German, the following lyrics represent both the original and translated text.

**Count in a Gray Coat**

**Verse 1**
Over ancient walls, there on the Rhine,
The count in the gray coat now falls asleep.

No crown, only the wind in his hair,
Before the electors' decree.

Fifty years before fortune found him,
An inconvenience for the Holy Land.


**Refrain**
Between the Enns and the Mur, the green heart,

Baptized in blood on the Marchfeld ore.

Austria, Styria, the golden seed,
A foundation of bold deeds.

Vienna, the anchor, firm in the stream of time,
Ready for eternity.

**Verse 2**

*(Softly)* Engelbert in Cologne saw only the light,
He did not see the cage or the end.

The Rhine bowed before Habsburg power,
The interregnum is now in store.

No sultan, only the distant Mongols in sight,
Rudolf gradually restores peace to the empire.

**Refrain**
Between the Enns and the Mur, the green heart,

Baptized in blood on the Marchfeld ore.

*(On the Marchfeld Ore)*
Austria, Styria, the golden seed,
A foundation of bold deed.

Vienna, the anchor, firm in the stream of time,
Ready for eternity.

**Bridge**
The castle crumbles, the name remains,
Like sand slipping through our fingers.

No gold, no victory, only God's favor,
In the end, life is just art.

**Outro**
Beneath Speyer Cathedral, in the eternal light,
Habsburg's dream will not be shattered.
