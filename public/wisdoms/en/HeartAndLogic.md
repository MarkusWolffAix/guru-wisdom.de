# The Servant of the Heart
## Why the Mind Must Not Belong to Itself

The mind is a powerful tool, an unparalleled abstraction machine. It orders the world, creates structures, and develops systems to make the complex chaos of existence comprehensible. Yet, for all its brilliance, pure intellect possesses no inherent moral compass. It does not abstract for its own sake—or at least, it shouldn't.

The true purpose of the mind is to serve the heart. All genuine insights, however profound and structured they may be, ultimately belong to love and human experience. Whether it makes sense to further abstract a thought or to refine a system is, at its core, a decision of the heart. If the heart learns something through this abstraction, if the individual grows, understands, or heals, then the intellectual process is good and valuable.

This truth can be observed on all levels—from personal self-reflection to global societal structures. A clear example of this is laws, bureaucracies, and governmental systems. They often originate from a deep human need: the protection of the vulnerable, the desire for justice, or concern for the common good. But as soon as these systems become too abstract, they lose touch with the people they are meant to help. They no longer reach us, but serve only themselves and their own organizational perpetuation.

A system that serves only itself is the clearest sign that the natural balance has been lost. It has degenerated into a pure abstraction, disconnected from the heart that once gave it meaning and life.

Ultimately, the same applies to society as to the individual: all our logic, all our deep reflection, and all our clever theories are merely vessels of glass as long as they are not imbued with a deeper, empathetic sense. The mind may map the world, it may construct frameworks and structures—but the heart must always decide where the journey leads.
