# Easter

## History, Origin, and Symbols

### 1. The Biblical Easter Story
Easter forms the foundation of the Christian faith and celebrates the victory of life over death. The story encompasses the last days of Jesus:

* **Palm Sunday:** Jesus enters Jerusalem on a donkey like a king and is greeted by the people with palm branches.

* **Maundy Thursday:** At the Last Supper, Jesus shares bread and wine as symbols of his body and blood. That same night, he is betrayed and arrested by Judas Iscariot in the Garden of Gethsemane.

* **Good Friday:** Jesus is condemned to death by the Roman governor Pontius Pilate and crucified on Golgotha.

* **Holy Saturday:** The day of rest in the tomb, mourning, and silence.

* **Easter Sunday:** Women discover the empty tomb. An angel announces the resurrection of Jesus, who then appears to his disciples.


### 2. The Tombstone and Its Meaning

A central element of the Easter story is the heavy stone that was rolled in front of the rock-hewn tomb. It carries two levels of meaning:

* **Historical and practical:** In ancient Judea, such a boulder protected the tomb from wild animals and grave robbers. In Jesus' case, it was also sealed with a Roman seal and guarded, for fear that the disciples might steal the body.

* **Theological and symbolic:** On Good Friday, the stone represents the irrevocable end, the coldness of death, and the separation from the living. Its removal on Easter morning changes everything: It did not happen so that Jesus could emerge, but so that people could see into the empty tomb. The stone thus becomes the ultimate proof of the resurrection and symbolizes that divine power overcomes even the most formidable earthly obstacles.

The exact origin of the word is not entirely clear, but there are three main theories, the last of which is closely linked to an old clothing tradition:

* **Dawn/East (most likely theory):** Derived from the Indo-European root *aus-* (to shine). It refers to the light of dawn, when the empty tomb was discovered, and the eastward direction that early Christians faced when praying.

* **Spring Goddess "Ostara" (myth):** A widespread but historically unsubstantiated theory states that the festival was named after a Germanic spring goddess.

* **The translation theory surrounding the baptismal festival:** In the early Church, many people were baptized at Easter. These newly baptized individuals wore an **alb** as a symbol of purity, innocence, and new life. The term derives from the Latin *albus* (white) or *tunica alba* (white undergarment). This white garment was worn throughout Holy Week. Researchers suspect that the Latin word *alba* (white) was mistakenly confused over time with the term for "dawn," from which the name of the feast may have originated.

### 4. Good Friday and Holy Saturday

The prefix **"Kar-"** comes from the Old High German word *kara*, meaning "lament," "sorrow," or "mourning."

* **Good Friday:** Literally "Friday of Mourning." It commemorates the crucifixion and is a legally protected "silent holiday" in Germany (e.g., with a ban on dancing).

* **Holy Saturday:** "Saturday of Mourning." It is the day of rest in the tomb without festive church services or altar decorations.

### 5. The Easter Egg
The egg as an Easter symbol combines practical, religious, and pagan roots:

* **Practical reason:** During the 40-day Lenten fast, eggs were not allowed to be eaten. To preserve the eggs that hens continued to lay, they were hard-boiled. To distinguish them from raw eggs, they were dyed with plant juices.

* **Christian symbolism:** The egg represents the tomb of Jesus—rigid and lifeless on the outside, yet new life bursts forth from within.

* **Universal symbol:** Thousands of years before Christianity, the egg symbolized spring, fertility, and new beginnings in many cultures.

The idea that a rabbit brings the Easter eggs is a blend of natural observations and Protestant tradition:

* **Fertility Symbol:** Rabbits are among the first animals to have abundant offspring in spring and were often seen near people in the fields during this time of year.

* **Protestant Invention:** Because Protestants rejected the Catholic blessing of eggs in church, they invented the child-friendly story of the Easter Bunny in the 17th century to explain the sudden appearance of the eggs.

* **Commercial Breakthrough:** Originally, depending on the region, there were other egg-bringers (such as the stork or fox). The rabbit finally became established worldwide in the 19th century due to the success of illustrated children's books and the rise of chocolate bunny production.
