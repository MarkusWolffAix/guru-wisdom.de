# Proserpina Sarcophagus
## Myth, Christian Reinterpretation, and Charlemagne

### Unabridged Summary: The Proserpina Sarcophagus

This text examines the Roman Proserpina Sarcophagus (3rd century AD), housed in the Aachen Cathedral Treasury, from three main perspectives: the ancient origin myth, its Christian-biblical reinterpretation, and its historical reuse as a tomb for Charlemagne.

#### 1. The Ancient Myth: The Rape of Proserpina

The central motif of the relief is the Roman explanatory myth for the seasons and the cycle of life:

* **The Rape:** Pluto, the ruler of the underworld, abducts the unsuspecting Proserpina in his chariot to his dark realm.


* **The Mother's Despair:** Ceres, the goddess of agriculture, searches so desperately for her daughter that she neglects her duties, and the crops on Earth wither.

* **The Compromise:** Because Proserpina ate pomegranate seeds (the food of the dead) in the underworld, she is bound to the realm of the dead. Jupiter brokers a compromise: Proserpina spends spring and summer on Earth (nature flourishes) and autumn and winter in the underworld (nature dies).

* **Meaning:** On ancient graves, the myth offered solace, as it symbolically represented the hope of life after death and constant rebirth.

#### 2. Biblical Analogies and Christian Reinterpretation
In the Middle Ages, the *Interpretatio Christiana* was applied: Pagan cultural heritage was reinterpreted within the context of the Christian faith, as it was seen as premonitions of divine truth. The analogies are striking:

* **Resurrection:** Proserpina's return from the underworld has been interpreted as a parallel to Jesus Christ's descent into the realm of the dead and his subsequent resurrection.

* **The Grain of Wheat:** The winter sojourn of the harvest goddess's daughter underground echoes Jesus' biblical metaphor (John 12:24): Only when the grain of wheat falls into the earth and dies can it bring forth new life.

* **Mater Dolorosa:** Ceres's profound grief for her child strongly recalls the Christian iconography of the grieving Mary (Mother of Sorrows) at the foot of the cross.

* **The Pomegranate:** This fruit has been given entirely new meaning in Christianity—as a symbol for the Church (many seeds united in one peel), eternal life, and the cleansing blood of Christ.


* #### 3. Historical Reuse: Charlemagne's Tomb

The use of the pagan sarcophagus in 814 for the Christian Emperor Charlemagne is a prime example of *spolia reuse* (reusing ancient plunder or spoils):

* **Logistics:** The transport of tons of fine Carrara marble from Rome to Aachen across the Alps and rivers was extremely complex and a powerful status symbol.

* **Ideology:** Charlemagne pursued the *Renovatio imperii Romanorum* (Renewal of the Roman Empire). Burial in a genuine Roman sarcophagus reinforced his political claim as the direct successor of the ancient Caesars.

* **Practical Implementation:** The sarcophagus was cleaned and consecrated before use to rid it of "pagan filth." The original lid, which is now missing, likely fell victim to this process, as it often bore pagan inscriptions.

* * **History of the Resting Place:** Charlemagne's sarcophagus did not serve him for eternity. The tomb was opened by later emperors (including Otto III and Frederick Barbarossa). In the 12th and 13th centuries, Charlemagne's remains were transferred to the golden Shrine of Charlemagne, and the empty sarcophagus later became part of the cathedral treasury.
