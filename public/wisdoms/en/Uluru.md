# Uluru Ayers Rock

## A Monument of Radical Empathy

Standing before Uluru in the Australian Outback today, one is not only looking at a geological wonder of stone, but at a monument of interconnectedness. Uluru is a place where the infinite depths of Earth's history meet the oldest uninterrupted spirit of humankind. The true key to understanding this place lies in **radical empathy** – a profound connection that recognizes humans, animals, and the Earth as a pulsating unity.

### Two Timelines: The Stone and Humanity

#### Geology: The Echo of Creation
Uluru is composed of arkose sandstone, the remnants of a mountain range (the Petermann Range) that eroded around **550 million years ago** – during the Cambrian Explosion. The sand was washed into a primeval sea. Around **400 million years ago**, these flat layers were uplifted almost vertically by immense tectonic forces. While these forces shaped the rock, the very first amphibians crawled from the seas onto land. The vertical grooves of the rock are the "breathing lines" of an Earth that has folded over eons.

#### Culture: The Oldest Continuous Civilization
The ancestors of the Anangu settled the continent around **65,000 years ago**. Human presence at Uluru itself has been documented for at least **30,000 to 40,000 years**. While Neanderthals still roamed Europe, the Anangu had already established their complex spiritual laws for survival in the desert. The myths of Uluru were already tens of thousands of years old when the pyramids of Giza were built in Egypt.

#### The Name and the European Encounter

#### William Christie Gosse
In 1873, the British-Australian surveyor **William Christie Gosse** (1842–1881) became the first European to reach the monolith. * **Origin:** Born in Hoddesdon, Hertfordshire (England), he emigrated to Adelaide with his family at the age of eight (1850).

* **The Sighting:** On July 19, 1873, he discovered the rock during an expedition and named it *Ayers Rock* in honor of Sir Henry Ayers, then Premier of South Australia. Gosse surveyed approximately 160,000 square kilometers of land but died of a heart attack at the young age of 38.

Today, **Uluru** is once again the officially preferred name. It is a proper name from the Pitjantjatjara language that has no direct Western translation, but it emphasizes the spiritual identity of the place. Out of respect for this significance, climbing the rock has been permanently prohibited since October 26, 2019.


* ### Radical Empathy: The Secret of the Shapeshifters

The mythology of the **Tjukurpa** (Dreamtime) is based on a radical empathy for all living things. A central example is the **Mala** (the hare-wallaby people).

* **The Real Animal:** In biology, the Mala (shaggy-wallaby) is a small, rabbit-sized, nocturnal marsupial that lives in the desert under spiny grass. On the mainland, it is now critically endangered.

* **The Ancestor Beings:** In the Tjukurpa, the Mala possess a dual nature. They are creation beings who possessed human consciousness, performed ceremonies, and formed the caves of Uluru by fleeing the evil dog spirit *Kurpany*. In traditional art, they are often not depicted figuratively, but rather through their paw prints or U-shaped symbols (seated figures).


* * **Empathy as a Survival Skill:** The "shape-shifting" of the ancestors is a metaphor for radical empathy. Those who have the mala as their totem consider it a brother. To survive in the harsh desert, one had to empathize so deeply with the animal that the boundaries between human and animal consciousness blurred.

* **The "Everywhen":** In the Tjukurpa, empathy also transcends time. The ancestral beings do not exist only in a distant past; their pulse rests in the rock and is palpable in the present.

### Lessons from Stone: Stories and Parallels

Uluru serves as a gigantic, "living history book." Every furrow, cave, and discoloration tells a moral or practical lesson:

* **Kuniya and Liru:** The battle between the Woma python woman and the snake man (Liru) left deep vertical cracks in the rock—the direct impact marks of their digging stick. * **Lungkata:** The blue-tongued lizard-man stole food and lied about it. He burned in a fire, and his ashes still form the dark stains on the rock's surface. It serves as a warning against greed and dishonesty.

#### Parallels to World Mythologies
Despite tens of thousands of years of isolation, striking similarities to Western history and the Bible can be found:

1. **Creation from the Formless:** As in Genesis or the Greek Chaos, order arises from a "desert and empty" primordial world through the journeys of the creation beings.

2. **Sacred Mountains as Lawgivers:** Uluru corresponds to Mount Sinai as a place where moral and social laws are manifested in the stone.

3. **Universal Morality:** The fables of Uluru utilize the same principles of punishment and knowledge as the parables of world religions (comparable to Sodom and Gomorrah or Ananias and Sapphira).


### Fire Awakens the Stone: Ritual and Presence

This radical empathy and deeply rooted mythology culminate in the ritual gathering at Uluru. When an Anangu elder sits at the foot of the mountain and shares traditional stories, the principle is: **"Fire awakens the stone."** This metaphor works on three levels:

1. **Visual:** The flickering light of the bonfire in the dark desert night casts shadows on the rock face, making the millennia-old cave paintings and crevices appear dynamic and alive—the stone seems to breathe.

2. **Cultural:** A history book made of stone must be "read." Lighting the fire marks the beginning of storytelling, drawing in the sand, and singing. The frozen history becomes fluid again and is passed on to the next generation.

3. **Spiritual:** The rock is not inert matter. The spiritual energy of the ancestral beings rests physically within the stone. The fire and the ritual act as a spark, calling forth this dormant energy and bringing it into the present. The "always-when" unfolds: The ancestors awaken and once again actively participate in the life of the community.
