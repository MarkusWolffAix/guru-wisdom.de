# Creation Myths of the World
## The Universal Parallels of the Origin of the World

It is a fascinating phenomenon of comparative mythology that geographically and temporally completely isolated cultures—from the Polynesians to the ancient Greeks, and on to the Egyptians and Sumerians—developed almost identical narratives to explain the origin of the cosmos.

At the heart of all these myths lies the archetype of the **“World Parents”**. The core motifs can be divided into five universal phases:

### 1. The Original Embrace (The Primal State)
In almost all of these traditions, the world does not initially exist as empty space, but as a solid, inseparable unity of heaven and earth, personified as loving or procreative deities.

* **Māori:** Papatūānuku (Earth Mother) and Ranginui (Sky Father) lie one on top of the other in an eternal, close embrace.

* **Greek:** Gaia (Earth Mother) and Uranus (Sky Father) form the same oppressive, permanent union.

* **Egyptian:** Geb (Earth God) and Nut (Sky Goddess) also rest inextricably upon one another (here, only with reversed gender roles).

* **Sumerian:** Ki (Earth) and An (Sky) initially exist together as an undivided, gigantic world mountain.

### 2. Imprisonment in Darkness
The direct result of this dense embrace, according to the myths, is the absolute lack of space and light for life.

* **Māori:** The divine children vegetate in complete darkness and confinement between their parents' bodies.

* **Greek:** Uranus forces their children (the Titans) deep into the dark belly of the Earth (Tartarus), where they are imprisoned and cannot emerge into the light.


### 3. The Violent Separation
To create space for evolution, light, and life, the unity of the world parents must be torn apart by a radical, often violent act of one of their sons. The supporting element (forest or air) separates above and below.

* **Māori:** Tāne Mahuta (God of the Forest) uses the power of his legs and arms to lift the sky upwards and separate the parents.

* **Greek:** The Titan Cronus brutally separates heaven and earth by castrating his father Uranus with a sickle.

* **Egyptian:** Shu (God of Air) inserts himself between his parents and lifts the sky (Nut) from the earth (Geb) with his hands.

* **Sumerian:** Enlil (God of Wind) splits the world mountain into heaven and earth with elemental force.


### 4. The Brother War and the Division of the World
After space was created, a period of familial chaos followed in the myths. The children of the gods (the forces of nature) fought for supremacy and divided the world realms among themselves. This explains the often hostile nature of the elements.

* **Māori:** The weather god Tāwhirimātea was enraged by the division and sent storms to punish his brothers (sea, forest, harvest). An eternal cycle of erosion, storms, and deforestation began.

* **Greek:** The Olympian gods overthrew the Titans, whereupon Zeus, Poseidon, and Hades divided the domains of sky, sea, and underworld among themselves. Here, too, conflicts among the gods shaped the extreme weather and natural events.


* **Greek:** ### 5. Humankind as Earth's Material
The creation of humankind is not described in these myths as a magical conjuring from nothing, but as a crafted act in which humans are formed directly from the physical substance of the earth.

* **Māori:** The forest god Tāne forms the very first woman (Hine-ahu-one) from the red clay of Papatūānuku and breathes life into her.

* **Greek:** The Titan Prometheus molds the first humans from the clay and water of the earth (Gaia).

**The Anthropological Conclusion:**

This complete examination of the commonalities makes it clear that myths are not random fantasy stories. They are the result of universal human observations: The sky "fertilizes" the earth with rain, whereupon the earth gives birth to life. Plants (the children) need light and space between earth and sky to grow. The parallels between Māori, Greeks, Egyptians, and Sumerians show that humanity, regardless of continent or era, has used the same logical and poetic language to explain the architecture of nature.

Ten thousand whispers in a fold,
Ten thousand pulses in a pulse,
The Sky Father and the sleeping Earth,
A tether of skin and breath,
In the stillness of the first embrace.

Shoulder to the bark, heels in the clay!

Tāne pushes the stars away!

A vertical sigh, a sudden tear!

The blue curve rises, the mountains appear!

Space for the wind, room for the wing!

Hear the first song the separation brings!

Shoulder to the bark, heels in the clay!
