# Fair Milk
## Conscious Nutrition for Body and Mind

Milk production per cow has been massively increased through breeding and concentrated feed, pushing the animals to their physical limits.

* **Natural State:** A mother cow produces approximately **4–8 liters** per day solely for her calf.

* **100 Years Ago (Around 1920):** An average cow gave approximately **7–10 liters** per day.

* **Today (High Yield):** The average in Germany is approximately **30 liters** per day. Top cows achieve over **60 liters**, and the world record is approximately **127.6 liters**.

* **The Healthy Ideal:** A yield of **15–25 liters** allows the cow to live a healthy life and meet her energy needs from grass and hay.

* ## 3. The Clear Advantage: Why the Long-Life Cow Is Economically & Ecologically Better
The long-life cow (12–15 years lifespan) outperforms the high-yield cow (5–6 years) in all sustainability factors:

### Economic Superiority:

* **Lower Fixed Costs:** The rearing costs of approximately €2,500 are depreciated over 10–13 years instead of just 3. This significantly reduces the cost per liter of milk.

* **Higher Profits:** Massive savings on veterinary costs (fewer inflammations/metabolic problems) and lower expenses for expensive imported concentrated feed.

* **Better Herd Management:** The farmer has to raise fewer calves and can sell surplus animals instead of using them as expensive replacements for prematurely matured animals.


* ### Ecological Superiority:

* **CO2 Efficiency & Methane Balance:** Considering the entire lifespan and the herd including offspring, the long-life cow system produces significantly less methane per liter of milk. While a high-yield cow often emits less methane per liter on a single day (dilution effect), this isolated picture is misleading: Because high-yield cows "wear out" quickly, the farmer must constantly raise new calves. These young animals produce methane for two to three years without producing a single liter of milk. The long-life cow drastically reduces these unproductive rearing phases.

* **Climate Protection through Grazing:** Long-life cows graze on **permanent grassland**. This protects soils that store enormous amounts of CO2, whereas high-yield cows often require arable feed (corn/soy), for the cultivation of which land must be plowed.

## 6. Why does the high-yield dairy cow even exist? (The systemic pressure)

If the long-lived cow is so superior, why does high-yield breeding dominate? Farmers don't choose this out of malice, but because of agricultural policy and economic conditions:

* **The remuneration system:** Dairies pay almost exclusively for the sheer **quantity** (liters). There is no "longevity bonus" on the regular market. Those who have bills to pay need liters in their tanks.


* **The Vicious Cycle ("Grow or Go"):** With historically low milk prices, the survival strategy was: "If I barely earn anything per liter, I have to produce more." This led to oversupply and further falling prices.

* **High Fixed Costs per Stall:** Modern barns cost millions. A 15-liter cow occupies the same expensive space as a 40-liter cow. Banks insist that the stall generate maximum returns to repay the loans.

* **Historical Focus:** After World War II, the political motto was: "Maximum yields at low prices" to feed the growing population independently of imports.

* **Global Availability of Cheap Feed:** The high-yield dairy cow only functions through the massive import of extremely protein-rich concentrated feed (soy). 50 liters per day could not be produced from domestic grass alone.

* **One-Sided Breeding Goals:** For decades, breeding associations blindly selected for "milk yield," while neglecting health and fitness traits that are harder to measure.

## 7. Exiting the System (How to Succeed in the Change)
The shift away from "cheap milk" must take place on three levels: on the farm, in politics/business, and with the consumer.

### A. The Transformation on the Farm (The Farmer)
The change is a multi-year process known as **"extensification"** or **"low-input strategy"**:

* **Changing Breeding Strategy:** Moving away from pure Holstein genetics and towards "utility crossbreeds" with robust breeds (Simmental, Jersey, Brown Swiss) for improved fitness and hoof health.

* **Changing Feeding ("Low-Input"):** Drastic reduction of expensive imported concentrated feed. The cows primarily eat grass and hay again. Milk production is declining, but feed costs are falling even more sharply.

* **Certification & Premium Markets:** Joining strong organic associations (Bioland, Demeter) or obtaining niche certifications ("Hay Milk PGI," "Pasture Milk") to secure guaranteed price premiums.

* **Direct Marketing:** On-farm dairies, milk vending machines, or community-supported agriculture (CSA) to retain added value on the farm.

### B. Systemic Change (Politics and Supermarkets)

* **Restructuring Subsidies:** Taxpayer money (EU agricultural policy) should not be distributed based solely on acreage, but must reward ecosystem services (pasture grazing, species conservation, and reduced use of concentrated feed).

* **True Cost Accounting:** The hidden environmental costs of conventional milk (nitrates in water, climate damage) must be factored into the price. Sustainable milk would then be the more economically viable option.

* **End the bait-and-switch tactics:** The ban on selling food below production costs must be fully enforced. Milk must not be a bargain-basement commodity.

* **Long-term contracts:** Dairies and retailers must offer farmers multi-year contracts with guaranteed fixed prices to ensure their livelihoods and provide them with the planning security needed for barn renovations.

### C. The influence of the consumer

* **Choose strong labels:** Associations such as Demeter, Bioland, Naturland, or specialized labels like "Pro Weideland" and "Heumilch g.t.S." guarantee better conditions than the EU organic label alone.

* **Consider the farming method:** For conventional milk, choose at least level 3 (outdoor access) or 4 (premium/organic), as these guarantee access to the outdoors and more space.

* **The "hidden" milk:** Also look for organic or pasture-raised milk quality in cheese, butter, yogurt, and quark (80% of the milk produced ends up here).


* * **Regional & Direct:** Support milk vending machines, farm shops, and concepts like Community Supported Agriculture (CSA).

## 8. Practical Example: The "Low-Input" Farm in Reality
A typical real-world scenario illustrates how this transition can be successful:

**The Old System:** Farmer Müller kept 100 high-yield Holstein cows (10,000 liters/year per cow). They were kept indoors year-round, fed expensive soy/corn feed, and incurred high veterinary costs. After five years, the cows were exhausted. Müller worked 70 hours/week, and profits were minimal.

**The Change:** Müller is now crossbreeding with robust breeds (Simmental/Jersey) and has stopped buying concentrated feed. The cows graze on pasture from spring to autumn and eat hay in winter.

**The Result (The New System):**

* **Milk Yield Reduced:** Only 6,000 liters instead of 10,000 liters per cow.

* **Profit Increased:** Since grass grows almost for free, feed costs decrease by 70%. The animals are more robust, and veterinary costs are halved.

* **Long Lifespan:** The cows live to be 10 to 12 years old; significantly less breeding is required.

* **Fair Price:** Thanks to the hay milk/organic quality, Müller now receives 60 cents instead of 35 cents per liter from the dairy.

*Result:* Although 40% less milk is produced, the farmer has higher profits, healthier animals, less workload, and has successfully overcome the constraints of the system.

## 9. Conclusion
"Cheap milk" is economically short-sighted and ecologically costly. Switching to long-lived cows, recognizable by labels like **Demeter, Bioland**, or **Animal Welfare Standard 4**, is the only way to achieve sustainable agriculture. A small surcharge of approximately **30 cents** in the store ensures the survival of farmers, protects the climate, and safeguards the health of the animals.
